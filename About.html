Hispanic-Serving Institutions (HSI) and Undergraduate STEM Education

Our research site is South University which serves over 30,000 students. It is also an institution that serves a student population that is predominately Hispanic and over half of our students are first generation, the first of their families to graduate college. South University’s College of Education is leading this project in collaboration with faculty in STEM Colleges. This is our Track 3 grant, which is our second grant focused on interdisciplinary professional development lesson study groups of faculty that draw explicit attention to the role of academic literacy in the development of two engineering courses and one introduction to physics course for STEM majors.

ASHE

The Association for the Study of Higher Education was founded in 1976 in Washington, DC. Members are graduate students seeking master's or doctoral degrees in education or other social sciences. Membership also extends to University Faculty members. To support researchers, ASHE hosts annual conferences to provide a space for researchers to present their work.

Grant

The HSI STEM Servingness research is supported by a U.S. National Science Foundation (NSF) continuing grant, the HSI Institutional Transformation Project: STEM Undergraduate Education through a Hispanic Student Success Framework. In alliance with the NSF’s statutory mission, this project aims to develop an HSI Servingness Framework that can be applied to STEM disciplines. To accomplish this, the grant team has focused on understanding pedagogy development through evidence-based teaching approaches, student peer mentoring, and professional growth that enhances the student experience. Further, the grant's overall goal is to improve student learning, sense of belonging, and overall success, while also giving attention to retention and graduation rates, to increase student diversity in STEM. 

Project Goals

The project will successfully complete the following tasks:

  1. Development of an HSI Student Servingness Framework.
  2. Pedagogical development of engineering courses focused on Computational Literacy, which includes professional development of graduate students and faculty.
  3. Review of current policies, practices, and structures for undergraduate students with a focus on diversity, equity, and inclusion.
  4. Development and subsequent expansion of Peer Mentoring Program and TA Teaching Academy.

The HSI Student Servingness Framework will allow for institutional change through curriculum changes, policy review, and expanding peer mentoring and other activities. Expected outcomes using the proposed multi-faceted approach are:

  1. Increase the retention, graduation and persistence rates of the students.
  2. Improve the students self-efficacy and sense of belongingness.
  3. Give faculty opportunities to develop innovative, equity-centered evidence-based best practice teaching approaches that enhance student learning and equity in the classroom.
  4. Provide students with marketable skills to be successful in the professional world. The results will be disseminated through a website, conference presentations and peer reviewed publications, and university workshops.

Our innovative cross-disciplinary partnership between STEM and Education faculty will improve student learning and self-efficacy by:

  1. Developing new methods of instruction.
  2. Implementing associated curricular changes focused on computational literacy through evidence-based teaching approaches and STEM faculty coaching.
