About Our Research

Our research site is South University which serves over 30,000 students. It is also an institution that serves a student population that is predominately Hispanic and over half of our students are first generation, the first of their families to graduate college. South University’s College of Education is leading this project in collaboration with faculty in STEM Colleges. 

This is our Track 3 grant, which is our second grant focused on interdisciplinary professional development lesson study groups of faculty that draw explicit attention to the role of academic literacy in the development of two engineering courses and one introduction to physics course for STEM majors.

About the Grant

The HSI STEM Servingness research is supported by a U.S. National Science Foundation (NSF) continuing grant, the HSI Institutional Transformation Project: STEM Undergraduate Education through a Hispanic Student Success Framework. In alliance with the NSF’s statutory mission, this project aims to develop an HSI Servingness Framework that can be applied to STEM disciplines. To accomplish this, the grant team has focused on understanding pedagogy development through evidence-based teaching approaches, student peer mentoring, and professional growth that enhances the student experience. Further, the grant's overall goal is to improve student learning, sense of belonging, and overall success, while also giving attention to retention and graduation rates, to increase student diversity in STEM.

Enhance Education

Development and subsequent expansion of Peer Mentoring Program and TA Teaching Academy

What is HSI?

An HSI is a federally classified institution that meets a full-time undergraduate enrollment threshold of 25% Latinx and 50% Pell Grant eligibility.

Servingness

As Garcia, Nunez, and Sansone (2019) suggested, servingness is a multidimensional conceptual framework consisting of academic and non-academic outcomes. Several indicators of academic outcomes for servingness include persistence, graduation, transfer, course completion, and labor market outcomes. Nonacademic indicators include the development of academic self-concept, leadership identity, racial identity, critical consciousness, graduate school aspirations, and civic engagement.
